CFA Level I Ethics: A 2026 Study Guide for the Make-or-Break Section

15-20% of the exam. Most candidates score lowest here. Here is exactly how to approach it.

July 2026•11 min read

By the Sleek Academia tutoring team

menu_bookPart of our Complete CFA Level I Prep Guide → | Also read: 20-Week Study Plan →

Every CFA Level I candidate knows Ethics matters. The CFA Institute weights it at 15-20% — the highest single topic area on the exam. But most candidates do not realize how profoundly different Ethics is from the other nine topic areas until they face their first mock exam.

Unlike Financial Statement Analysis or Fixed Income — where you calculate a number and know whether you got it right — Ethics questions test situational judgment. The answer choices are not right versus wrong; they are most appropriate versus less appropriate. The difference between passing and failing Ethics often comes down to wording precision, not conceptual understanding.

This guide covers everything you need to approach the CFA Level I Ethics section in 2026: the Code and Standards structure, the most frequently tested scenarios, the exam traps that trip up even prepared candidates, and a study approach that actually works for this unique topic.

Why Ethics Decides More Than Its Weight Suggests

Here is something many prep providers do not emphasise enough: the CFA Institute uses Ethics scores to resolve borderline pass/fail decisions. If your total score falls within a narrow band around the Minimum Passing Score (MPS), the Ethics section becomes the tiebreaker. A strong Ethics performance can push you over the line; a weak one can sink an otherwise passable performance across the other nine topics.

This makes Ethics the highest-leverage topic on the exam. A 70% score in Ethics is worth more than 70% in any other topic, because of how the scoring is structured. Treat it accordingly.

The Code of Ethics and Seven Standards — What You Actually Need to Know

The CFA Institute Code of Ethics is the overarching framework. It lays out six ethical principles — integrity, competence, diligence, respect, and professionalism. But the exam tests the Standards of Professional Conduct at a granular level. There are seven standards, each with multiple sub-sections:

Standard I — Professionalism

This covers four situations: knowledge of the law (I-A), independence and objectivity (I-B), misrepresentation (I-C), and misconduct (I-D). The most frequently tested sub-standard is I-B (Independence and Objectivity). Exam questions often present scenarios where an analyst receives a gift from a company they cover, or where a manager pressures an analyst to issue a favourable rating. The correct response is always to disclose the conflict and decline the gift. There is no such thing as a gift that is "small enough to ignore" under the CFA Standards — any gift that could reasonably influence objectivity must be rejected.

Standard II — Integrity of Capital Markets

Material non-public information (II-A) and market manipulation (II-B). The key exam pattern: you are told about a situation where a candidate learns something material about a company from a non-public source. The correct answer is always to not act on it and to not pass it along. Even "indirect" use of MNPI — making a recommendation based on a tip without explicitly naming the source — still violates the standard.

Standard III — Duties to Clients

The longest and most heavily tested standard. It covers loyalty and prudence (III-A), fair dealing (III-B), suitability (III-C), performance presentation (III-D), and preservation of confidentiality (III-E). Fair dealing is particularly nuanced — it requires that investment actions be taken in a manner that does not disadvantage any client. "First come, first served" is acceptable only if you have disclosed the policy in advance. Changing recommendations must be communicated to all clients simultaneously, not selectively.

Standard IV — Duties to Employers

Loyalty (IV-A) and additional compensation arrangements (IV-B). Exam scenarios focus on two patterns: leaving a firm and taking work product (you cannot take client lists or proprietary research models without permission), and accepting compensation from third parties (must be disclosed and consented to by your employer).

Standard V — Investment Analysis and Recommendations

Diligence and reasonable basis (V-A), communication with clients (V-B), and record retention (V-C). The exam tests diligence through scenarios where an analyst makes a recommendation without sufficient research. The principle: your recommendation must have a reasonable basis, and you must communicate the risks and limitations alongside your conclusions.

Standard VI — Conflicts of Interest

Disclosure of conflicts (VI-A), priority of transactions (VI-B — client trades come before personal trades), and referral fees (VI-C). Referral fees are a frequent exam subject — you must disclose any compensation you receive for referring a client to another professional, in writing, before the referral relationship begins.

Standard VII — Responsibilities as a CFA Institute Member or CFA Candidate

Conduct as members and candidates (VII-A) and reference to the CFA designation (VII-B). Common exam trap: you cannot say "I am a CFA charterholder" until you have completed the work experience requirement. You also cannot disclose exam results or content — sharing the topics you found hardest on the February 2026 exam is a violation of the Candidate Pledge.

The Three Most Common Ethics Exam Traps

After reviewing hundreds of Level I Ethics questions, we have identified three patterns that cause the most errors:

Trap 1: The "It's Just Small" Fallacy. Exam questions frequently describe a scenario with minor-seeming violations — a small gift, an informal favour, a casual mention of non-public information. The correct answer is almost always that a violation occurred. The CFA Standards do not make exceptions for scale.

Trap 2: Disclosure Alone Is Not Enough. Many candidates answer that a violation is acceptable "if disclosed." But disclosure is not a cure-all. For some violations, disclosure must be accompanied by corrective action — you must refuse the gift, not just disclose you received it. For conflicts of interest, disclosure must be made explicitly before the transaction, and the client must provide informed consent.

Trap 3: Not Understanding the "Supervised Person" vs. "Member" Distinction. The Standards apply differently depending on whether you are a member of the CFA Institute (or a candidate) versus a supervised person who is not a member. Questions test whether you know your compliance responsibilities extend to people you supervise, even if they are not charterholders.

How to Study Ethics for CFA Level I

The most common mistake candidates make with Ethics is saving it for last, assuming it is the easy, reading-heavy section. In reality, Ethics has one of the lowest average scores on the exam. Here is a better approach:

1. Read the Official Curriculum, Not Just Summaries

Third-party prep providers offer condensed Ethics notes, and they are useful for review. But the CFA Institute's official Ethics curriculum includes case studies and application examples that are directly relevant to how questions are framed. Read the official text at least once. The cases in the CFAI materials are not decoration — they demonstrate the reasoning style the exam expects.

2. Do Not Start Practice Questions Until You Understand the Framework

Ethics practice questions are valuable, but only if you approach them systematically. Do not try to memorise answer patterns — try to understand why each correct answer is correct and why each incorrect answer is a lesser choice. This matters because exam questions are rephrased every year. A candidate who memorised "always decline the gift" will still struggle if the question is framed as "your manager told you to accept it — what do you do?"

3. Target 100+ Ethics Questions Before Exam Day

Aim for at least 100 Ethics practice questions, ideally spread across your study period rather than concentrated in one session. The CFA Institute online question bank is the best source. After each question, write down which sub-standard was being tested — this builds the mental map that lets you identify the correct principle on exam day without hesitation.

4. Use the "Most Appropriate" Decision Framework

For every Ethics question, ask three questions in order: (1) Is there a violation of the Code or Standards? (2) Whose duty was violated — the member's duty to clients, to employers, or to the profession? (3) What corrective action should be taken? This framework aligns with how the CFA Institute structures its question writing and will help you stay on track when answer choices feel ambiguous.

Integrating Ethics Into Your 20-Week Study Plan

Rather than saving Ethics for the last two weeks, introduce it after you finish Quantitative Methods. Ethics content is independent of other topics, so there is no prerequisite knowledge required. Spreading Ethics across your study period — 15-20 minutes per session, several times per week — creates spaced repetition that is far more effective than a single intensive Ethics weekend.

We recommend this timeline:

  • Weeks 3-4: Read the official Ethics curriculum cover to cover. Do not rush.
  • Weeks 5-14: Complete 6-8 Ethics practice questions per week alongside your other topic practice. Track which standards you miss most.
  • Weeks 15-17: Complete a concentrated block of 30-40 Ethics questions. Review the case studies in the CFAI curriculum again.
  • Weeks 18-20: Include Ethics questions in every mock exam. Record your Ethics score separately and track improvement.

If you follow this timeline, you will enter the exam having seen Ethics in nearly every study session — not as a crammed add-on, but as a continuous thread throughout your preparation.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of the CFA Level I exam is Ethics?

Ethical and Professional Standards carries 15-20% of the total exam weight — making it the single highest-weighted topic area on CFA Level I.

What are the seven CFA Standards of Professional Conduct?

Standard I: Professionalism; Standard II: Integrity of Capital Markets; Standard III: Duties to Clients; Standard IV: Duties to Employers; Standard V: Investment Analysis and Recommendations; Standard VI: Conflicts of Interest; Standard VII: Responsibilities as a CFA Institute Member or CFA Candidate.

Is CFA Level I Ethics hard?

Yes — Ethics has consistently been one of the lowest-scoring sections on the CFA Level I exam. The questions test nuanced interpretation of the Standards rather than rote memorisation, and the answer choices are deliberately close.

How should I study for CFA Level I Ethics?

Read the official CFA Institute Ethics curriculum (not just third-party summaries), practice at least 100 Ethics questions, and focus on understanding the reasoning behind each correct and incorrect answer. Ethics rewards depth over breadth.
