NextGen Bar Exam 2026: What's Changing and How to Prepare

The biggest bar exam overhaul in a generation — explained without the noise

June 2026 • 9 min read

By the Sleek Academia tutoring team

If you are sitting for the bar in 2026 or 2027, you are studying during a transition that the National Conference of Bar Examiners (NCBE) has described as the most significant redesign of the exam in decades. There are two separate changes happening at once, and most candidates conflate them. The first is the arrival of the NextGen Uniform Bar Exam. The second is a slimming-down of the legacy Multistate Essay Examination (MEE). Whether one, both, or neither applies to you depends entirely on where you take the exam and when. This guide separates the two cleanly so you can build the right plan.

The Two Tracks: NextGen UBE vs. the Legacy Exam

Not every jurisdiction switches to NextGen at the same time. In July 2026, a first wave of jurisdictions adopts the NextGen UBE, while the rest continue administering the legacy exam — but with a trimmed MEE subject list. Knowing which track you are on is the single most important thing to confirm before you spend a dollar on prep.

Who is on NextGen in July 2026

The announced July 2026 launch jurisdictions include Connecticut, Idaho, Maryland, Missouri, Oregon, and Washington, along with several U.S. territories (Guam, the Northern Mariana Islands, Palau, and the Virgin Islands). Most remaining U.S. jurisdictions have signaled they will transition across 2027 and 2028. Because adoption dates continue to move, always verify directly with the board of bar examiners for the jurisdiction where you intend to be admitted.

What the NextGen UBE Actually Tests

The redesign reflects a simple idea: the bar should measure what new lawyers actually do, not just what they can memorize. That shows up in two ways — a shorter list of doctrinal subjects, and an explicit set of skills.

The 8 doctrinal subjects

From July 2026 through at least February 2028, the NextGen UBE tests eight core subjects:

  • Business associations and relationships
  • Civil procedure
  • Constitutional law
  • Contract law
  • Criminal law and the constitutional protections of accused persons
  • Evidence
  • Real property
  • Torts

Note what is gone from the standalone subject list: secured transactions, conflict of laws, trusts and estates, and family law as discrete essay topics. Family law is a special case. During the transition window, family law concepts appear in a performance task and may show up in integrated question sets, but they are tested with provided legal resources rather than from pure memory.

The 7 foundational skills

This is the genuinely new layer. The NextGen UBE names seven lawyering skills it will assess: legal research, legal writing, issue spotting and analysis, investigation and evaluation, client counseling and advising, negotiation and dispute resolution, and client relationship and management. You cannot cram these the way you cram a rule statement — they are practiced, not memorized, which is why integrated question sets and performance tasks now carry roughly half the exam's weight.

The Legacy MEE Change (If Your Jurisdiction Has Not Switched)

If you are sitting in a jurisdiction still on the legacy exam in July 2026, you are not off the hook for changes. The NCBE has posted an updated MEE subject outline that removes four subjects effective with the July 2026 administration: Conflict of Laws, Family Law, Trusts and Estates, and Secured Transactions. That narrows your essay-prep surface area meaningfully — but only if your commercial outline or tutor has actually updated for it. Many candidates will waste hours in 2026 outlining subjects that can no longer appear. Confirming this one detail is among the highest-return moves in your prep.

How to Study for a Moving Target

A transition year rewards candidates who plan around the format, not just the content. Here is the sequence we walk students through:

  • Confirm your track first. Email or check your board of bar examiners and pin down NextGen vs. legacy for your exact sitting before buying any materials.
  • Match your materials to the format. If you are on NextGen, prioritize prep that drills integrated question sets and performance tasks, not just black-letter outlines.
  • Train the skills deliberately. Build weekly reps of legal research and writing under timed conditions — the skills weighting means these now move your score as much as doctrine.
  • Prune dead subjects. On the legacy track, remove the four discontinued MEE subjects from your rotation and reinvest that time in high-frequency topics.
  • Simulate the new clock. The 1.5-day, three-session structure changes pacing and stamina demands; practice in that rhythm before exam week.

Frequently Asked Questions

When does the NextGen Bar Exam start?

The NextGen Uniform Bar Exam debuts in July 2026 in a limited group of jurisdictions, including Connecticut, Idaho, Maryland, Missouri, Oregon, and Washington, plus several U.S. territories. Most remaining jurisdictions plan to transition during 2027 and 2028.

Which subjects are being removed from the MEE in July 2026?

For jurisdictions still giving the legacy bar exam in July 2026 and later, the updated Multistate Essay Examination outline removes four subjects: Conflict of Laws, Family Law, Trusts and Estates, and Secured Transactions.

How many subjects are on the NextGen Bar Exam?

The NextGen UBE tests eight doctrinal subjects, down from fourteen on the legacy exam: business associations and relationships, civil procedure, constitutional law, contract law, criminal law and constitutional protections, evidence, real property, and torts. It also assesses seven foundational lawyering skills.

How is the NextGen Bar Exam scored?

The NextGen UBE reports a single scaled score on a 500 to 750 scale. The score combines standalone multiple-choice questions (about 49 percent), integrated question sets (about 21 percent), and performance tasks (about 30 percent), and is equated for consistency across administrations and jurisdictions.
