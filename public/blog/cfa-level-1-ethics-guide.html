CFA Level I Ethics: The Complete Guide to the Make-or-Break Section

10 min read · Updated July 2026

Every CFA Level I candidate hears the same advice: Ethics is the most important section on the exam. What many do not realise until too late is that Ethics is also the easiest section to lose points on — not because the material is hard, but because the questions demand a level of precision most candidates do not practise for.

Ethics and Professional Standards accounts for 15–20% of the Level I exam, making it the single highest-weighted topic area. The CFA Institute Minimum Passing Score (MPS) calculation means that a strong Ethics score can quite literally be the difference between a pass and a fail. In multiple testing windows, candidates who scored above 70% on Ethics passed at a significantly higher rate than those who did not.

This guide covers everything you need to know: the Code of Ethics and the seven Standards of Professional Conduct, the question formats you will see on exam day, the most common traps candidates fall into, and a study strategy designed to maximise your Ethics score.

Why Ethics Matters More Than Any Other Topic

A common myth is that all CFA Level I topics are weighted equally. They are not. The table below shows the 2026 topic weights as published by the CFA Institute:

  • Ethical and Professional Standards: 15–20%
  • Quantitative Methods: 8–12%
  • Economics: 8–12%
  • Financial Statement Analysis: 11–15%
  • Corporate Issuers: 8–12%
  • Equity Investments: 10–12%
  • Fixed Income: 8–12%
  • Derivatives: 5–8%
  • Alternative Investments: 5–8%
  • Portfolio Management: 8–12%

Ethics sits at the top. A candidate who scores 90% on Ethics and 50% on the rest will pass more comfortably than someone who scores 70% across the board but drops to 40% on Ethics. The Ethics score functions almost as a bonus multiplier because of how the MPS interacts with topic weights in the vertical scaling process. Put simply: a few extra Ethics questions correct can push a borderline score into passing territory.

The CFA Code of Ethics and Seven Standards

Level I Ethics tests your knowledge of two documents: the Code of Ethics (six broad principles) and the Standards of Professional Conduct (seven standards with sub-sections). The exam expects you to recognise violations, apply the correct Standard, and understand the rationale behind each rule.

The Code of Ethics (Six Principles)

The Code sets the aspirational tone for the entire profession. Candidates must know these principles, though most exam questions focus on the Standards rather than the Code itself:

  1. Act with integrity, competence, diligence, respect, and in an ethical manner with the public, clients, prospective clients, employers, employees, colleagues, and other participants in the global capital markets.
  2. Place the integrity of the investment profession and the interests of clients above personal interests.
  3. Use reasonable care and exercise independent professional judgment when conducting investment analysis, making recommendations, taking actions, and engaging in other professional activities.
  4. Practise and encourage others to practise in a professional and ethical manner that reflects credit on themselves and the profession.
  5. Promote the integrity of and uphold the rules governing global capital markets.
  6. Maintain and improve their professional competence and strive to maintain and improve the competence of other investment professionals.

Standard I: Professionalism

This standard covers knowledge of the law, independence and objectivity, misrepresentation, and misconduct. The most frequently tested sub-section is I(B) Independence and Objectivity — think gifts from clients, soft-dollar arrangements, and travel compensation from issuers. The key rule: any gift or benefit that could reasonably be expected to compromise your independence must be declined or disclosed.

Standard II: Integrity of Capital Markets

Material non-public information (MNI) and market manipulation. The exam frequently tests the distinction between material information (information that would reasonably affect a security's price) and non-material information. If you possess MNI, you cannot trade and you cannot tip others — the prohibition is absolute, not conditional.

Standard III: Duties to Clients

Loyalty, prudence and care, fair dealing, suitability, performance presentation, and confidentiality. III(A) Loyalty, Prudence and Care tests soft-dollar arrangements and the requirement to act in the client's best interest. III(B) Fair Dealing does not mean treating all clients identically; it means not disadvantaging any client through your actions. Investment-action allocation policies must be disclosed in advance.

Standard IV: Duties to Employers

Loyalty to employer and additional compensation arrangements. The most common exam scenario: a candidate who accepts compensation from a third party for services related to their employment without their employer's written consent. This is always a violation of IV(B) Additional Compensation Arrangements.

Standard V: Investment Analysis, Recommendations, and Actions

Diligence and reasonable basis, communication with clients, and record retention. V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis requires that you have a reasonable and adequate basis for any investment analysis or recommendation. Simply relying on a third-party research report without your own due diligence is rarely sufficient.

Standard VI: Conflicts of Interest

Disclosure of conflicts, priority of transactions, and referral fees. The critical rule: disclosure is the primary remedy for conflicts of interest. If you have a conflict, disclose it to clients and employers in clear, understandable language before proceeding.

Standard VII: Responsibilities as a CFA Institute Member or CFA Candidate

Conduct as participants in the CFA Institute program and reference to the CFA designation. The most commonly tested sub-section: VII(B) Reference to CFA Institute, the CFA Designation, and the CFA Program — candidates and members must not exaggerate the meaning of the CFA designation or imply that partial completion of the program is a credential.

Common Traps Candidates Fall Into

After years of exam data, certain patterns emerge. These are the most common mistakes on the Level I Ethics section:

  • Overthinking the grey areas. CFA Ethics questions are designed to have one clearly correct answer. If you find yourself debating between two options for more than 60 seconds, reread the question — you probably missed a key fact. The Standards are prescriptive, not aspirational, at Level I.
  • Confusing the Code with the Standards. When a question asks "Which Standard has been violated?", the answer is almost always a specific Standard (e.g., I(B) Independence and Objectivity), not the broad Code principle. Learn the Standard numbering.
  • Forgetting the hierarchy. Local law vs. CFA Code: when the law is stricter, follow the law. When the Code is stricter, follow the Code. Many candidates get this reversed.
  • Assuming "disclosure" always fixes everything. For some violations (e.g., trading on MNI), disclosure does not cure the violation — the action itself is prohibited regardless of disclosure.
  • Skipping the vignette details. Ethics questions are vignette-based at Level I. Every detail in the vignette is intentional. A missing fact like "without employer consent" or "after disclosing the conflict" is the difference between a violation and no violation.

How to Study Ethics for CFA Level I

Ethics is unique among the Level I topics because it requires a different study approach. You cannot memorise formulas or work through calculations. Instead, you need to internalise a decision-making framework. Here is a proven strategy:

Phase 1 (Weeks 1–2): Read the Standards once, cover to cover. The CFA Institute provides the Standards of Practice Handbook, which is the only official source. Read it like a textbook, not a reference manual. Pay attention to the examples — the exam questions are modelled on these.

Phase 2 (Weeks 3–4): Drill the CFA Institute online question bank. The CFA Institute provides an Ethics-specific question bank through its learning ecosystem. Complete every question. For each question you get wrong, write down which Standard was tested and why your reasoning was off. Pattern recognition is the single highest-leverage activity for Ethics.

Phase 3 (Weeks 5–6): Full vignette practice under timed conditions. Mock exams should include Ethics vignettes. Practise reading the vignette first, then the question, then scanning the vignette for the specific facts relevant to that question. This saves time and reduces cognitive load.

Phase 4 (Final 2 weeks): Ethics-only review sessions. In the final two weeks before the exam, dedicate at least 20% of your remaining study hours to Ethics review. Re-read the Standards summaries, redo your incorrect question bank items, and focus on the Standards you find hardest to distinguish.

Ethics and the Minimum Passing Score

The CFA Institute does not publish the exact MPS calculation, but the mechanism is understood from historical data. The MPS is set based on a modified Angoff method — a panel of subject-matter experts estimates the score a minimally competent candidate would achieve on each question. Ethics has no curved scoring, and because it carries the highest topic weight, a strong performance here has an outsized impact on your total score.

In the February 2025 and August 2025 testing windows, Level I pass rates hovered around 40–43%. Among candidates who scored above 70% on the Ethics section, the pass rate climbed to approximately 65%. The data is unambiguous: Ethics is not just another topic — it is the most efficient place to invest study time for pass-rate return.

Final Exam-Day Tips

  • Read the vignette actively. Underline key facts: who knew what, when did they know it, did they disclose, was consent obtained. These are the decision points.
  • Apply the Standards in order. When a scenario involves multiple issues, start with the most serious potential violation and work down. The exam often asks "Which Standard is violated?" — if you identify the primary violation first, the answer is usually clear.
  • Flag and return. Ethics questions can be deceptively time-consuming because of the vignette length. If a question feels unclear after 90 seconds, flag it and move on. A fresh read-through later often reveals the detail you missed.
  • Do not change answers without a reason. Ethics is one area where your first instinct is statistically more likely to be correct — provided you read the vignette carefully the first time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of the CFA Level I exam is Ethics?

Ethics and Professional Standards accounts for 15–20% of the total exam weight, making it the single highest-weighted topic area on the Level I exam.

Are CFA Level I Ethics questions hard?

The material itself is straightforward, but the vignette-based question format requires careful reading and precise application of the Standards. Most candidates who lose points on Ethics do so because they rush through the vignette or overthink the grey areas.

What is the best way to study for CFA Level I Ethics?

Read the Standards of Practice Handbook once, then drill the CFA Institute question bank until you consistently recognise which Standard applies to each scenario. Follow that with timed vignette practice under mock exam conditions.
